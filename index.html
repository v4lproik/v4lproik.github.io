Presentation

From French government hacker to founder, CTO, and blockchain engineer.

I was recruited by the French government at 20yo through international CTF competition and worked there full time while completing my engineering degree. Offensive security was the right starting point: it exposed me to a wide range of architectures and taught me to reason across the OSI layers, from high-level interfaces and application logic down through cryptography, networking, low-level protocol work, and hardware security.

That range made blockchain a natural place to work, because the engineering only really makes sense if you can think across protocol design, infrastructure, execution, and market structure at the same time. Today I focus on Ethereum and Solana distributed systems, especially cross-chain infrastructure, trading systems, and market design around prop AMMs and CLOB-style execution, where performance and cost optimisation are central. Right now that means working on opinion-market infrastructure and transaction-landing reliability for a company I work with, treating execution quality as a core product problem rather than an afterthought.

I like zero-to-one work. I have scaled engineering from 0 to 10 engineers, taken products and teams from idea to operation, led due diligence across multiple investors, and helped raise more than $13M from pre-seed through seed with a number of considered S-tier investors. Doing that well is less about any single trait than about combining product sense, engineering depth, speed, hiring, team alignment, and execution under constraint, while keeping maintainability, engineering quality, and security intact.

Rust and Go are the languages I use most, including work at the L1 and validator-client layers on both Ethereum and Solana. A lot of that work sits close to node operators, in critical environments where private-key handling, enclave design, failure domains, performance tuning, timing games, and revenue optimisation are first-order concerns. I have modified base clients to improve APY and operator returns, and introduced new features required by the ecosystems I was building in. Terraform, Terragrunt, and Ansible are part of my daily infrastructure work, and I am usually at my best on the complex technical problems that other teams would rather not own.

I work across French, English, and Spanish at C1, Danish at B2, and Korean in progress. Clear communication matters to me as much as technical depth: I am comfortable moving between engineers, operators, investors, and leadership teams, and framing the same problem at the right level for each of them.

Current mode

I do my best work when I can take a system end to end: research, specifications, implementation, infrastructure, and production delivery. Most of that work today sits in institutional-grade blockchain engineering for node operators and trading systems, especially across Ethereum and Solana, where client performance, validator economics, execution paths, and reliability have a direct effect on uptime and revenue. A lot of the current work is around opinion markets, propAMM and CLOB-style infrastructure, CU optimisation, and transaction-landing reliability, and principal-level engineering across contracts, backend services, data flows, observability, and infrastructure. Good blockchain systems have to be designed with the full set of stakeholders in mind: users, node operators, builders, and the operational constraints each group carries. Understanding L1 roadmaps matters just as much, because durable infrastructure usually comes from seeing where the protocol is going before the rest of the stack catches up. The next project moving toward public release is Yieldia.

Careers

Chronology of work

The domains changed over time, but the operating mode stayed consistent. I started with internet communities, release tooling, and small teams, moved through offensive security and distributed data systems, then into platform engineering, protocol work, and blockchain infrastructure. The common thread is straightforward: work close to production, close to failure modes, and close enough to the system that performance, reliability, and judgment all matter.

Execution

Founder and hands-on CTO

Built a company from zero to 20 people and grew engineering from zero to 10. I handled fundraising and investor due diligence through pre-seed and seed while staying embedded in architecture, hiring, product definition, and delivery. The role mattered because it forced business judgment and technical judgment to stay aligned.

01 Company building Fundraising, hiring, architecture, and delivery without stepping away from the engineering work.
Staking

Kiln: validator platforms and operator-grade systems

At Kiln I led backend engineering around validator infrastructure and validator-as-a-service systems across Ethereum and other proof-of-stake networks. The work ranged from shared Go and Rust foundations and internal CI to SLAs, alerting, monitoring, resilience, and the practical boundary between software teams and operator reality. I was also already working directly on validator economics, improving APY and operator returns, and shipping custom modifications where the default client or stack behaviour was not good enough.

02 2022 to 2023 / Kiln Validator platforms, shared software foundations, custom operator mods, and APY-sensitive reliability work.
Protocol

Ethereum Foundation: Lighthouse and Beacon Chain internals

Through the Ethereum Protocol Fellowship, I worked on Lighthouse and Beacon Chain internals in Rust with a focus on consensus-layer behaviour, performance, metrics, and protocol-facing debugging. I also worked on the implications of moving Ethereum toward single-slot finality, especially the effect on hardware requirements, validator location, and the broader operator footprint of the chain. It made the protocol and operator interface much more concrete: what matters in client code stops being abstract once validator behaviour and uptime are on the line.

03 2023 / Ethereum Protocol Fellowship Consensus-client internals, SSF impact analysis, metrics, and performance work close to the chain.
Platform

Yinkozi: principal engineering for security products

At Yinkozi I led engineering and architecture for two security products across Golang, Scala, React, gRPC/Protobuf, Terraform, Kubernetes, Prometheus, and ELK. It was principal-level work in the practical sense: shaping the system, shipping the platform, and helping the team keep quality high while moving fast.

04 2019 to 2022 / Yinkozi Architecture, multi-cloud delivery, platform execution, and team leadership in one surface area.
